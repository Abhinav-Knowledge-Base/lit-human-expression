# Poetry: The Art of Rhythmical Composition

**Poetry** is a form of literature that uses aesthetic and often rhythmic qualities of language—such as phonaesthetics, sound symbolism, and metre—to evoke meanings in addition to, or in place of, a prosaic ostensible meaning.

## 1. Major Types of Poetry
- **Epic Poetry:** Long, narrative poems concerning a serious subject containing details of heroic deeds and events significant to a culture or nation.
    - *Examples:* [Homer's Iliad](file:///d:/Miscellaneous/Books/Western/1.%20Ancient%20Greece/Iliad.md), [Valmiki's Ramayana](file:///d:/Miscellaneous/Books/Indian/1.%20Ancient%20&%20Classical/Ramayana.md).
- **Lyric Poetry:** A formal type of poetry which expresses personal emotions or feelings, typically spoken in the first person.
    - *Features:* Musicality, brevity, and emotional intensity.
- **Narrative Poetry:** Poetry that tells a story. While epics are narrative, not all narrative poems are epics (e.g., ballads).
- **Dramatic Poetry:** Poetry written specifically to be spoken or acted (e.g., Shakespeare’s plays are largely dramatic poetry).

## 2. Structural Elements
- **Stanza:** A grouped set of lines within a poem.
- **Metre:** The rhythmic structure of a verse or lines in verse.
- **Rhyme Scheme:** The pattern of rhymes at the end of each line of a poem or song.
- **Blank Verse:** Unrhymed iambic pentameter.
- **Free Verse:** Poetry that does not rhyme or have a regular metre.

## 3. Historical Evolution
- **Classical:** Strict adherence to form and metre (Greek and Sanskrit traditions).
- **Romantic:** Focus on the "spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings."
- **Modernist:** Breakage of traditional forms, fragmentation, and experimentation (e.g., T.S. Eliot).
